- The current issue is more likely related to power delivery, startup torque, or hardware-level constraints rather than a completely incorrect overall structure

### 2. Reflected on current engineering weaknesses
Based on today's discussion, identified that the real gap is not just "building systems," but:
- Debug capability
- Engineering robustness
- Reliability of output
- Depth of understanding behind design choices

This led to a clearer distinction between:
- Making something run once
- Building something stable, explainable, and reusable

### 3. Reframed the internship/lab-entry problem
Analyzed the problem from both sides:

From my side:
- I want a real internship/research experience
- I want exposure to higher-level projects
- I want to prove I can contribute meaningful work

From a lab/teacher side:
- They want low-risk, reliable, and manageable students
- They want someone who can reduce workload rather than create more of it
- They need stable people who can continue working and gradually become productive

Conclusion:
- Entering a stronger lab is not only about enthusiasm or having a basic project
- What matters is whether I can provide stable and reliable productivity

### 4. Adjusted the strategy for the next stage
The previous mindset was too close to:
- "Try to contact a stronger lab directly and hope for an opportunity"

Today's adjustment is:
- First become a person worth taking in
- Strengthen engineering depth and reliability
- Prioritize building real productivity and trust
- Focus more realistically on local/project-based opportunities first, while keeping stronger external platforms as a medium-term goal

## Key Learnings
### 1. Debug ability is a core engineering skill
Engineering value is not only in building a system, but in being able to:
- Localize problems
- Control variables
- Separate layers
- Turn vague symptoms into concrete evidence

### 2. Robustness matters more than surface completion
A system that "runs once" is not enough.
A stronger project should be:
- Reproducible
- Stable
- Clearly documented
- Understandable to others

### 3. Reliable output is highly valuable
For labs and teams, reliability includes:
- Consistent progress
- Task closure
- Clear reporting
- Low management cost
- Long-term stability

### 4. Career planning should be based on supply-demand logic
Instead of only asking what I want, I should also ask:
- What kind of person does a lab actually want?
- What abilities are scarce and valuable?
- What can I realistically offer right now?
